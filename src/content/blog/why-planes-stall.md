Every student pilot learns the same uncomfortable truth early: when a plane starts to fall, you push forward. Not back. Forward.

Everything in your body says pull. Your instinct is to pitch the nose up, climb away from the ground, fight gravity. But that instinct is exactly what causes a stall — and once you're in one, it makes everything worse.

## What a Stall Actually Is

A stall isn't an engine failure. The engine can be running perfectly and the plane can still stall. It's not purely about speed either, though the two are related.

A stall happens when the **angle of attack** exceeds its critical value.

[Recall from the last post](/blog/how-wings-generate-lift): angle of attack is the angle between the wing and the incoming airflow. As you increase it — pitching the nose up — you generate more lift. But there's a limit. Beyond roughly 15–20 degrees (depending on the airfoil), the smooth flow over the top of the wing can no longer stay attached to the surface. It separates.

When flow separates, circulation collapses. Lift doesn't decrease gradually — it disappears. Almost instantly.

That's a stall.

## The Boundary Layer Problem

To understand why flow separates, you need to know about the **boundary layer** — the thin region of air right next to the wing's surface where viscosity matters and the airflow slows considerably.

Under normal conditions, the boundary layer stays attached. The air slows near the surface, but the pressure gradient keeps pushing it forward along the wing.

At high angles of attack, that gradient reverses. Near the trailing edge, pressure is actually increasing as you move backward along the wing. The slow-moving air in the boundary layer runs out of momentum. It stalls — stops moving forward, starts reversing — and the entire flow field over the top of the wing breaks down.

This is called **flow separation**, and it's why stall recovery feels urgent. Once separation starts near the trailing edge, it propagates forward quickly. The wing is no longer a wing.

## The Recovery You Have to Override Your Instincts to Execute

My aircraft flight dynamics professor called this the "stick pusher problem." You're falling, and the correct response is to push the stick forward — further into the fall. Every instinct you have says don't do that.

The only way to recover from a stall is to reduce angle of attack. That means pushing the nose forward — toward the ground.

When the plane is close to the ground, pointing the nose down seems like it defies every rule of survival. But there is no other path out. The wing has stopped generating lift, and the only thing that restores it is restoring attached flow. Attached flow requires reducing angle of attack. Reducing angle of attack means pitching down.

The recovery sequence: push forward, level the wings, add power, climb. In that order. Not power first — power without a flying wing means you're accelerating into the ground with more energy.

## What Happens When One Wing Stalls First

If one wing stalls before the other — common in a banked turn where the inside wing is moving slower — you get a **spin**. The stalled wing drops. The other wing, still generating lift, causes a rotation. The aircraft enters a descending spiral that compounds the original stall and adds a gyroscopic twist to the problem.

Spin recovery follows the PARE procedure: Power off, Ailerons neutral, Rudder opposite to the direction of spin, Elevator forward. The principle is the same as basic stall recovery — angle of attack first, everything else second. But the execution is harder because you're also managing rotation.

## Why This Pattern Keeps Appearing

There's a recurring structure in aerodynamics: the intuitive model and the physical reality diverge, and the divergence is exactly where things go wrong.

Bernoulli gets you started. Circulation explains the mechanism. Boundary layer theory explains the failure mode. Each layer reveals something the previous one obscured.

Stall is the point where a casual understanding of lift becomes dangerous. The pilot who only knows "stalls happen at low speed" will pull back when the nose drops. That's the wrong move, and it follows logically from an incomplete model.

Knowing the actual mechanism — that it's angle of attack, not airspeed, that's the real variable — is what gets you to push forward instead. That's not a small distinction.
